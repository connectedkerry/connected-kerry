## What we're seeing

Transport infrastructure and services are frequently planned around the assumption that users are independent drivers. This default shapes everything from where bus stops are located to how timetables are structured to whether footpaths exist at all.

The result is that large groups of people — who have always existed and whose needs have always been present — are marginalised or excluded from systems that are nominally public.

## Who this affects

**Older residents:** As driving becomes difficult or impossible with age, access to services, family, and community becomes dependent on transport options that may not exist or may not be suitable.

**Children and young people:** Children travelling independently to school, activities, or part-time work may have no safe or practical option if public transport is limited and walking routes are unsafe.

**People with disabilities:** Inaccessible stops, lack of low-floor vehicles, and the absence of real-time information in accessible formats all create barriers for passengers with mobility or sensory impairments.

**People without car access:** Whether through cost, choice, or circumstance, a significant proportion of the population does not drive or does not have regular access to a car. Rural transport systems that assume otherwise exclude these people from economic and social participation.

<div class="callout callout--amber">
  <strong>A note on framing</strong>
  <p>This campaign does not frame car-dependency as a personal failing. Many people in Kerry depend on cars because they have no practical alternative. The question is whether that dependency is the result of geography and genuine necessity, or the result of planning decisions that could have been made differently.</p>
</div>

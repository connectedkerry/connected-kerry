## What we're seeing

Transport investment decisions are frequently framed around cost — the cost of building infrastructure, the cost of running services, the cost per passenger journey. What is less often counted is the benefit side of that equation: what better transport access delivers for households, businesses, communities, and the wider economy.

This campaign collects evidence and frames the case for the benefits that improved local transport would bring in a Kerry context.

## Areas of benefit

**Household savings:** A household that can manage without a second car — or without a car at all — can save a significant sum annually. In a rural county where car ownership is often treated as unavoidable, this framing is underused.

**Local business:** Better access to town centres and commercial areas by foot, cycling, and bus supports retail and hospitality. Visitors and residents who arrive without a car are often noted by businesses as higher-spending customers.

**Social connection and wellbeing:** For older residents, young people, and those without cars, transport access is directly linked to social participation, mental health, and independence. Isolation linked to poor transport is a real cost that rarely appears in transport appraisals.

**Environmental quality:** A modal shift away from private car use at local level contributes to reduced emissions, improved air quality in town centres, and reduced road wear — benefits that are shared by the whole community.
